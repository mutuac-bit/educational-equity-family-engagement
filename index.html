Can Family Engagement Close the Achievement Gap?

A statistical investigation using Generalized Linear Models to test whether parent involvement in education can reduce socioeconomic disparities in student outcomes

25,391 Students Analyzed
18% Stronger Effect for Low-Income
4 Statistical Models

Executive Summary

High-income students in the U.S. are 1.7 times more likely to earn mostly A's compared to low-income students‚Äîa 25.8 percentage point gap that persists across grade levels. This project investigates a critical question: Can family engagement interventions help level the playing field?

Key Findings

  • Compensatory Effect Confirmed: School engagement reduces at-risk probability 18% MORE for low-income students (Œ≤ = -0.20, p < 0.01)
  • Strongest Protective Factor: Homework involvement reduces at-risk odds by 41% per standard deviation increase (OR = 0.59)
  • Robust Across Methods: Findings replicated across multinomial logistic, binary logistic, Poisson, and LDA models
  • Policy-Relevant: Targeted interventions for low-income families yield higher returns than universal programs

The Achievement Gap is Real

But here's the promising finding: Low-income families CAN and DO engage when barriers are removed, and engagement has stronger protective effects for disadvantaged students.

Data & Methodology

Data Source

I analyzed the NCES Parent and Family Involvement in Education (PFI) Survey, combining 2016 and 2019 waves for a total of 25,391 observations after cleaning. The dataset captures:

  • Academic outcomes: Student grades (4 categories), at-risk status (binary), attendance (count)
  • Engagement measures: School activities (8 types), homework involvement, cultural enrichment
  • Socioeconomic indicators: Household income (12 levels), parent education, family structure
  • Control variables: Grade level, disability status, race/ethnicity, school type

Why Multiple Models?

Educational outcomes are multifaceted. Student grades aren't just "good" or "bad"‚Äîthey exist on a spectrum. Absences are count data that can't go negative. At-risk status is binary. Each outcome type requires a different statistical model from the GLM family:

Outcome Type Model Why
Student Grades 4 unordered categories Multinomial Logistic No natural ordering between categories
At-Risk Status Binary (yes/no) Binary Logistic Dichotomous outcome, probability bounded 0-1
Days Absent Count data Poisson Regression Non-negative integers, no upper bound
Grades (validation) Multivariate LDA Different assumptions for robustness check

Statistical Foundation: Probability Theory in Action

Throughout this project, probability theory provided the foundation for every modeling decision:

  • Maximum Likelihood Estimation: Coefficients are chosen to maximize P(data | parameters)
  • P-values: Under the null hypothesis (Œ≤ = 0), what's the probability of observing effects this extreme?
  • Confidence Intervals: 95% CIs capture the plausible range of effect sizes given sampling variability
  • Standard Errors: Quantify uncertainty in estimates due to finite sample size

Feature Engineering

I created three composite engagement measures to capture different dimensions of family involvement:

# School Engagement: Count of 8 activities (0-8 scale)
school_engagement = attend_event + volunteer + general_meeting + 
                    pta_meeting + parent_teacher_conf + fundraising + 
                    committee + counselor

# Homework Involvement: Standardized composite (mean=0, SD=1)
homework_involvement = scale(
  (homework_days + homework_hours + homework_help) / 3
)[,1]

# Cultural Enrichment: Weighted sum of weekly + monthly activities
cultural_enrichment = (story + crafts + games + projects + sports_home) +
                      (library + bookstore) / 4 + dinners / 7

Handling Missing Data

Challenge: 46.7% of race/ethnicity data and 31.3% of school choice variables were missing.

Solution: Created an "Unknown" category for race/ethnicity to preserve sample size. Excluded school choice variables (entire 2016 wave missing‚Äîconfounded with survey year).

Validation Strategy

To ensure findings weren't artifacts of modeling choices, I used:

  • Stratified 10-fold cross-validation: Maintains class proportions in each fold
  • Separate test set (25%): Held out for final evaluation
  • Multiple model comparison: Multinomial vs. LDA convergence validates assumptions
  • Multicollinearity checks: All engagement measures had r < 0.35

Engagement Patterns by Socioeconomic Status

This 0.88 activity gap likely reflects structural barriers (time constraints from multiple jobs, transportation challenges, less welcoming school environments) rather than differential interest. Key insight: Low-income families CAN engage more when barriers are removed.

The Compensatory Effect in Action

Moving from low to high engagement:

  • Low-income students: Success rate increases from 32% ‚Üí 44% (+12 pp)
  • High-income students: Success rate increases from 54% ‚Üí 67% (+13 pp)

While high-income students benefit slightly more in absolute terms, the relative benefit is much larger for low-income students (37.5% relative increase vs. 24.1%).

Lessons from Model Failures: The Class Imbalance Problem

‚ö†Ô∏è When "94% Accuracy" Means Nothing

The binary logistic model achieved 94.1% accuracy in cross-validation but completely failed on the test set (ROC-AUC = 0.21). Why?

The Lesson: With severe class imbalance (only 5.7% at-risk), the model learned to always predict the majority class. This minimizes overall error but is useless for identifying students who actually need help.

Key Takeaway: Overall accuracy masks performance failures when classes are imbalanced. Always check:

  • Confusion matrices (not just overall accuracy)
  • Class-specific metrics (sensitivity, specificity)
  • Test set ROC curves (CV performance can be misleading)

Silver Lining: Despite prediction failures, the coefficient estimates remained valid and interpretable‚Äîthey tell us which factors matter, even if the model can't make useful predictions with extreme imbalance.

Model Convergence = Robust Findings

When multinomial logistic regression and Linear Discriminant Analysis‚Äîmodels with different assumptions (parametric likelihood vs. multivariate normality)‚Äîyield nearly identical results, it provides strong evidence that findings are robust and not artifacts of modeling choices.

Policy Implications & Future Directions

Actionable Recommendations for Schools

Evidence-Based Strategies

  1. Prioritize Homework Involvement Programs (Strongest effect: OR = 0.59)
    • Provide structured homework help sessions
    • Train parents in effective support strategies
    • Set clear, achievable expectations
  2. Target Low-Income Families (Compensatory effect: 18% stronger)
    • Focus outreach on disadvantaged communities
    • Remove barriers: transportation, childcare, scheduling
    • Universal programs risk being captured by high-resource families
  3. Focus on Accessible Activities
    • Parent-teacher conferences (no special resources needed)
    • School event attendance (builds community)
    • General meetings (low commitment threshold)
  4. Support Students with Disabilities (Highest risk factor: OR = 1.61)
    • Generic engagement programs won't address specialized needs
    • Require targeted interventions beyond family involvement

Why This Matters

These findings directly contradict the assumption that engagement programs help everyone equally. The compensatory effect means:

  • Targeting matters: Don't spread resources thin with universal programs
  • Equity requires differential investment: Give more support where it helps most
  • Measurable impact: 18% reduction in at-risk odds for low-income students is substantial

Limitations & Future Research

Study Limitations

  • Observational design: Cannot establish causation definitively
  • Self-reported data: Subject to social desirability bias
  • Cross-sectional: Cannot assess cumulative effects over time
  • Class imbalance: Binary model couldn't effectively predict minority class

Future Directions

  • Causal inference: Propensity score matching or natural experiments (e.g., policy changes)
  • Mechanism analysis: WHY does engagement help disadvantaged students more?
  • Longitudinal models: Track students over time for cumulative effects
  • Cost-effectiveness: Which activities provide best "bang for the buck"?
  • Class balancing techniques: SMOTE, cost-sensitive learning for binary predictions

Reflection on Learning Objectives

Objective 1: Probability as Foundation of Statistical Modeling

This project deepened my understanding of how probability theory underpins every step:

  • Model building: Constructed regression models for categorical, binary, and count outcomes
  • Parameter estimation: Maximum likelihood finds coefficients that maximize P(data | parameters)
  • Inference tools: P-values, confidence intervals, and standard errors quantify uncertainty
  • Validation: Cross-validation assesses how well models generalize to new data

Objective 2: Applying Appropriate GLMs

Matching models to response types was critical:

  • Multinomial logistic: For unordered categorical grades (4 levels)
  • Binary logistic: For dichotomous at-risk status‚Äîlearned about class imbalance pitfalls
  • Poisson regression: For count data (days absent)‚Äîappropriate when variance ‚âà mean
  • LDA: Validated findings under different assumptions (multivariate normality)

The convergence of results across methods demonstrates that findings are robust to statistical assumptions.
